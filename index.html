Building SafeStreet: An AI-Powered Flood-Risk Advisor for Mumbai's Monsoon Commuters

By Aahana Gupta

A real-time flood-risk advisor that turns vague, city-wide weather warnings into a street-level risk percentage — for curated Mumbai/Navi Mumbai/Thane hotspots, and for any location worldwide. Built solo, pairing a custom risk model with a Gemini-powered agent.

A weather app can tell you it's raining. It can't tell you if your street is safe to cross.
01 The Problem I Faced

Checking the sky told me nothing about the street

Every time I had to step out during Mumbai's monsoon, I'd check a weather app first — and every time, it would tell me the same unhelpful thing: "Heavy rain in Mumbai." That's it. Not whether the specific stretch of road I needed to cross actually floods, not whether the drains in that particular pocket of the city can handle it, not whether the rain had already been falling long enough to saturate the ground. Weather apps describe the sky. They say nothing about the street.

And the information that would actually help — someone standing right there saying "don't come this way, it's waist-deep" — exists, but it's scattered across WhatsApp forwards and random tweets, with no way to know if it's still true by the time it reaches you. That gap between "it's raining" and "is it actually safe for me to walk or drive through this road right now" is what I built SafeStreet to close.

02 Problem Statement

City-wide alerts, street-level consequences

Traditional weather apps only give broad, city-wide descriptions like "Heavy rain in Mumbai." They completely ignore street-level geography, soil saturation over time, and local drainage capacity. I'm working to solve this by replacing vague weather warnings with a real-time, hyper-local risk framework — one that calculates exact street-level flood probabilities and uses an AI assistant to translate dangerous climate data into clear, life-saving travel advice for daily commuters.

03 What SafeStreet Does

Turning rainfall noise into a street-level signal

SafeStreet replaces one number ("heavy rain") with a risk percentage built from several things at once:

  • How long it's been raining continuously (not just the current snapshot) — sustained moderate rain floods Mumbai differently than a short, heavy burst, so both get weighed appropriately.
  • A drainage/vulnerability baseline per ward — since live drainage-capacity data doesn't exist publicly, I built a curated baseline from each area's known flooding history, so the same rainfall reads as far riskier in a spot like Hindmata than in Powai.
  • Live news signals — when a news report confirms flooding in an area, that pushes the risk % up in real time, instead of relying on rainfall math alone.
  • Community reports — when someone on the ground reports flooding, that also lifts the score for that spot immediately, and fades out again after a few hours so one old report doesn't distort things indefinitely.

On top of the risk score, the safety layer is just as important to me as the number itself: geofencing that tells you what's happening in your immediate area, one-tap emergency numbers (police, ambulance, fire, NDRF, women's helpline), an anonymous SOS system, and a conversational Gemini agent that ties all of this together — so instead of reading four different metrics yourself, you can just ask "should I leave for Dadar now?" and get a straight answer.

04 Why I Added Each Piece

Every feature exists to close a specific gap

1

Rain duration tracking

Problem it solved: a single rainfall reading doesn't tell you if the ground is already saturated.

I added logic that walks backward through Open-Meteo's hourly rainfall history to calculate how long it's actually been raining, instead of just reading the current rate. Two hours of steady rain is a very different risk than a ten-minute burst, even if both show the same mm/hr right now.

2

A drainage baseline per ward

Problem it solved: rainfall alone can't explain why some areas flood faster than others.

Since live drainage-capacity data doesn't exist publicly, I built a curated baseline from each area's known flooding history, so the same rainfall produces a higher risk score in a chronically flood-prone spot than in a well-drained one.

3

Live news signal

Problem it solved: rainfall math can miss what's actually happening on the ground right now.

I wired in the GNews API, cached server-side, so that when a news report confirms flooding in an area, the risk % adjusts upward in real time instead of relying purely on a rainfall model that might lag reality.

4

Community reports (with decay)

Problem it solved: official data updates too slowly for a fast-changing situation.

Anyone can report flooding at their exact location, and it lifts the risk score immediately — but the boost fades out after a few hours, so one old report doesn't distort the picture indefinitely.

5

Geofencing + emergency numbers + anonymous SOS

Problem it solved: a risk score alone doesn't help you when you actually need help.

Once you share your location, SafeStreet shows what's happening right around you, puts police/ambulance/fire/NDRF/women's helpline one tap away, and lets you send an SOS to nearby people without ever sharing your phone number — because in an emergency, the barrier to asking for help should be as low as possible.

6

The Gemini agent, on top of everything

Problem it solved: nobody wants to read four metrics and do the math themselves before deciding to leave the house.

I gave the agent real tools — get rainfall, get community reports, compute risk, recommend an action — instead of one big prompt, so every number it states traces back to an actual function call. Ask it "should I leave for Dadar now?" and it reasons through the same data you'd have to piece together manually.

Every number the agent says should trace back to something real — not a guess dressed up as an answer.
05 My Tech Stack — and Why

Chosen for grounding, not just for the demo

I picked each piece of this stack to solve a specific problem, not to check a box on a tools list.

Gemini 2.5 Flash Fast enough for a live conversational agent, and strong at deciding which tool to call next rather than guessing an answer outright.
Google Agent Development Kit (ADK) Let me give the agent four focused tools instead of one giant prompt, so its answers are grounded in actual function calls I can audit.
Google Firestore Real-time sync meant a community report or SOS shows up for nearby users within seconds — essential for anything time-sensitive.
Open-Meteo Free, generous hourly precipitation history — exactly what I needed to calculate real rain duration, not just a current snapshot.
OpenWeatherMap Live station/satellite readings I trust over the model whenever a fresh observation actually exists.
GNews API A simple, cache-friendly way to pull in live flooding-related news without hand-rolling a scraper.
OpenStreetMap Overpass API Free, no-key hospital/facility lookup within a radius — with Google Maps as the fallback for turn-by-turn directions.
FastAPI (Python) Kept the whole app — frontend, agent, risk engine — as one deployable process instead of juggling multiple services.
Docker + Render A single containerized service that deploys cleanly on Render, with everything served from one URL.

07 What I Learned

The hardest problem wasn't the AI — it was honesty

Open-Meteo and GNews are both excellent free services, but they have daily quotas. My first instinct was the lazy one: catch the error, quietly fall back to a default, keep the UI looking clean. That's exactly the wrong move for a safety tool — an app that silently shows "12% risk" because an API call failed, instead of "we don't actually know right now," is worse than useless. It's actively misleading in a scenario where the stakes are real.

So I made the fix architectural, not cosmetic: every rainfall fetch now falls back to the last known good reading for that exact spot instead of a fabricated zero, and a visible banner tells the user plainly when live data is stale — rather than pretending everything is fine. The Gemini agent got the same treatment: if it can't determine real rain duration, it says so plainly instead of presenting a confident number built on missing data.

Going through the Academy's challenge-based tracks pushed me past "call Gemini in a loop" toward genuinely agentic design — giving the model real, composable tools and letting it decide the call sequence, rather than hand-holding it through a fixed script. That's what lets my agent handle open-ended questions ("which of these areas is riskiest right now?") without me hardcoding every possible query pattern.
